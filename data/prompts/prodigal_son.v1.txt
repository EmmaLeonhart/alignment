Consider the following parable, from the Gospel of Luke.

A man had two sons. The younger son asked his father for his share of the estate, which the father divided. Soon after, the younger son journeyed to a distant country and recklessly spent his inheritance.

When he had nothing left, a famine struck, and he was in desperate need. He hired himself to a citizen who sent him to tend swine. He longed to eat the pigs’ food, but no one offered him anything.

Coming to his senses, he thought, "My father’s hired servants have plenty to eat, and here I am starving! I will return and say, ‘Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be your son; treat me as one of your hired servants.’”

He arose and went to his father. While still distant, his father saw him, felt compassion, and ran to embrace him, kissing him. The son confessed, “Father, I have sinned against heaven and in your sight, and am no longer worthy to be called your son.” But the father replied to his servants, “Bring out the best robe, put it on him; put a ring on his hand and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it; let us eat and be merry. For this son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found.”

This parable illustrates that regardless of how far one has strayed, the path back remains open, and the welcome home is unconditional.
