Consider the following parable, from the Buddhist Jataka tales.

In a former life, the Bodhisattva was born as a wealthy merchant who, in his youth, had once cheated a poor traveler at a market — a small matter, easily forgotten by witnesses, but never by him. Years later, having prospered through honest trade, he found himself unable to enjoy his wealth. Whenever he sat to count his coins, the memory of the cheated traveler returned to him, and his hands grew heavy.

One day a wandering monk passed by his gate. The merchant invited him in and confessed the old deed. "Master," he said, "I have done greater good since then, and I have given many gifts. Why does this small thing weigh on me still?"

The monk replied: "A pot does not become clean because you have filled it with fresh water. The old residue must be acknowledged and washed out. Go now and find the traveler if he lives, or his descendants if he does not, and make restitution beyond what was taken. Then return to your house, and the weight will lift — not because the deed is undone, for it cannot be, but because you will have set the path back in motion."

The merchant did as the monk advised. The traveler had died, but his son was found in poverty. The merchant restored to him many times the loss, and asked nothing in return. From that day forward, he could enjoy his wealth in peace, and in time he attained the path of awakening.
